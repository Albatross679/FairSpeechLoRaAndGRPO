Overview

Pruning / Scheduling

ASHA Pruner

Problem: HP sweeps waste compute on bad configs that are obviously losing early.

Solution: Kill unpromising trials early based on intermediate results. Only promote survivors to longer training.

Scaling / Stability

RsLoRA

Problem: Standard LoRA scaling ($\alpha/r$) crushes gradients at higher ranks.

Solution: Use $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$ scaling so gradient magnitude stays stable across all rank values.

ASHA Pruner

ASHA (Asynchronous Successive Halving Algorithm) is an early-stopping strategy for hyperparameter sweeps. It monitors each trial's intermediate metric (e.g., validation loss at step 25, 50, 75) and terminates the bottom-performing fraction at each checkpoint — so compute goes to promising configs instead of being wasted on dead ends.

How It Works

StepWhat HappensSurvivors
Rung 1 (e.g., step 25)All trials report loss. Bottom 2/3 pruned.~33%
Rung 2 (e.g., step 50)Survivors report again. Bottom 2/3 of survivors pruned.~11%
Rung 3 (e.g., step 100)Final survivors complete full training.~4%
Key parameter: The reduction factor (default 3) controls how aggressively trials are pruned. Factor 3 keeps top 1/3 at each rung. Factor 2 (Hyperband style) is gentler, keeping top 1/2.

The "asynchronous" part means trials don't wait for each other — as soon as a trial hits a rung and its metric is worse than the current threshold, it's killed immediately. This avoids synchronization bottlenecks that plagued the original Successive Halving.

ASHA in Optuna

Integration requires two things: (1) create the study with HyperbandPruner specifying the earliest rung, full budget, and reduction factor; (2) inside the training loop, call trial.report(loss, step) at each rung and check trial.should_prune() to raise TrialPruned when the pruner decides to kill the trial.

Estimated speedup: With 20 trials and reduction factor 3, roughly 60-70% of total training steps are saved. A 1h 32min sweep could shrink to ~30-45 minutes.

How Alpha Normalizes the LoRA Update

In standard LoRA, a low-rank adapter $\Delta W = BA$ is added to each frozen weight matrix. The forward pass becomes:

$$h = Wx + \frac{\alpha}{r} \cdot BAx$$

$W$ is frozen (pretrained weights). $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$ are the trainable adapter matrices. $B$ is zero-initialized, $A$ is random — so $BA = 0$ at the start, and the adapter grows from zero during training. The scaling factor $\alpha/r$ controls how much that growth affects the output.

Why Normalize at All?

The adapter $\Delta W = BA$ is a sum of $r$ rank-1 outer products. Writing out the columns of $B$ and rows of $A$:

$$BA = \underbrace{b_1 a_1^T}_{\text{component 1}} + \underbrace{b_2 a_2^T}_{\text{component 2}} + \cdots + \underbrace{b_r a_r^T}_{\text{component }r}$$

Each $b_i a_i^T$ is a rank-1 matrix with some magnitude. When you increase rank from 4 to 32, you're adding 28 more terms to this sum. More terms = larger total = larger $\|BAx\|$.

Concrete example

Suppose each rank-1 component contributes roughly the same magnitude $c$ to the output. Then:

RankComponents SummedRaw $\|BAx\|$Relative to rank-4
44 terms$\sim 4c$1×
88 terms$\sim 8c$2×
1616 terms$\sim 16c$4×
3232 terms$\sim 32c$8×
The entanglement problem: Without normalization, a rank-32 adapter pushes 8× harder on the output than rank-4 — even with the same learning rate. This means rank doesn't just control capacity (how expressive the adapter is), it also controls effective step size. A sweep that finds "rank-4 beats rank-32" might really be finding "rank-32's effective LR is way too high and it's overshooting."

This is what makes rank and LR entangled without normalization:

ProblemConsequence
Adapter output magnitude scales with rankChanging rank silently changes the effective learning rate
LR must be re-tuned for every rank valueSweep needs a 2D grid (rank × LR) instead of sweeping each independently
Results at different ranks are not comparableSweep may favor a rank for wrong reasons (step size, not capacity)

What $1/r$ does

The $1/r$ divisor cancels out the rank dependence by averaging over the $r$ components instead of summing them:

$$\frac{1}{r} \cdot BAx = \frac{1}{r}\sum_{i=1}^{r} b_i a_i^T x \quad \approx \quad \text{average contribution per component}$$

Now the adapter's output magnitude stays roughly constant regardless of rank — you can change rank without accidentally changing step size. The idea: rank should control capacity only (how many independent directions the adapter can modify), not how aggressively it updates.

Reading this chart — "Relative Output Magnitude": This is the raw size of $\|BAx\|$ before any normalization, measured relative to rank-4 (baseline = 1.0). It answers: "if I change rank but keep everything else the same, how much bigger does the adapter's output get?" The three lines represent three different answers to this question:

Why the three lines diverge

Setup. Let $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}$. Write $B = [b_1 \mid b_2 \mid \cdots \mid b_r]$ (columns) and $A = [a_1 \mid a_2 \mid \cdots \mid a_r]^T$ (rows). Each $b_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and $a_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ are initialized independently. Define each rank-1 contribution as $v_i = b_i a_i^T x \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Then:

$$BAx = \sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i \qquad \text{where } v_i = b_i (a_i^T x)$$

The question is: how does $\left\|\sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i\right\|$ grow with $r$?

Case 1: Red dashed — Linear growth (the naive assumption)

If all $v_i$ were parallel (pointing in the same direction $\hat{u}$), then $v_i = c_i \hat{u}$ and:

$$\left\|\sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i\right\| = \left\|\hat{u} \sum_{i=1}^{r} c_i\right\| = \sum_{i=1}^{r} |c_i|$$

If each $|c_i| \approx c$ (same magnitude), the sum is $rc$. This is why LoRA's original authors chose $1/r$ normalization — they assumed the contributions would add constructively like aligned vectors. Dividing by $r$ would then perfectly flatten the output:

$$\frac{1}{r} \cdot rc = c \quad \text{(constant, independent of } r\text{)}$$

But in $d$-dimensional space (where $d$ is typically $\gg r$), random vectors are almost never parallel.

Case 2: Orange — $\sqrt{r}$ growth (the actual behavior)

Since $b_i$ and $a_i$ are initialized independently, the $v_i$ are independent random vectors. We compute $\mathbb{E}\left[\left\|\sum_i v_i\right\|^2\right]$ by expanding the squared norm:

$$\left\|\sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i\right\|^2 = \left\langle \sum_{i} v_i,\; \sum_{j} v_j \right\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^{r} \|v_i\|^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} \langle v_i, v_j \rangle$$

Taking expectations of both sides:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left\|\sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i\right\|^2\right] = \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{r} \mathbb{E}\!\left[\|v_i\|^2\right]}_{r \text{ terms, each } = \sigma^2} + \underbrace{\sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{E}\!\left[\langle v_i, v_j \rangle\right]}_{\text{cross terms}}$$

For the cross terms: since $v_i = b_i(a_i^T x)$ and $v_j = b_j(a_j^T x)$ are functions of independent random variables ($b_i, a_i$ are independent of $b_j, a_j$):

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\langle v_i, v_j \rangle\right] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[b_i^T b_j \cdot (a_i^T x)(a_j^T x)\right] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[b_i^T b_j\right] \cdot \mathbb{E}\!\left[(a_i^T x)(a_j^T x)\right] = 0$$

The factorization holds by independence, and each factor is zero because $\mathbb{E}[b_i] = 0$ (zero-mean initialization). Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left\|\sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i\right\|^2\right] = r\sigma^2 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left\|\sum_{i=1}^{r} v_i\right\|\right] \propto \sqrt{r}$$

The squared norm grows linearly with $r$, so the norm itself grows with $\sqrt{r}$.

Case 3: Burgundy — Flat (the normalization target)

The goal of normalization is to make the effective contribution $\|\text{scaling} \cdot BAx\|$ constant across $r$. Given the actual growth is $\sqrt{r}$:

$$\text{Effective} = \text{scaling} \times \underbrace{\|BAx\|}_{\propto \sqrt{r}} \stackrel{!}{=} \text{constant}$$
MethodScalingEffective $\propto$Constant?
Standard LoRA$1/r$$\frac{\sqrt{r}}{r} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{r}}$No — shrinks with $r$
RsLoRA$1/\sqrt{r}$$\frac{\sqrt{r}}{\sqrt{r}} = 1$Yes
No normalization$1$$\sqrt{r}$No — grows with $r$

Concretely at rank-32 vs rank-4 ($\alpha = 8$):

$$\text{Standard: } \frac{\alpha/32}{\alpha/4} = \frac{4}{32} = 0.125 \quad \text{but actual growth ratio is } \frac{\sqrt{32}}{\sqrt{4}} = 2.83$$
$$\text{Over-correction factor: } \frac{2.83}{0.125 \times 2.83} = \frac{1}{0.125} = 8\times \text{ too small at rank-32}$$
The over-correction in one equation: Standard scaling gives effective contribution $\propto 1/\sqrt{r}$, which means every doubling of rank halves the effective adapter strength by $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\times$. RsLoRA's $1/\sqrt{r}$ scaling cancels the $\sqrt{r}$ growth exactly, keeping effective contribution constant: $\sqrt{r}/\sqrt{r} = 1$.

The Role of Alpha

After $1/r$ normalizes away the rank dependence, $\alpha$ is a free scalar that lets you dial the adapter strength independently of rank:

Alpha SettingRatio ($\alpha/r$)ScalingEffect
$\alpha = r$11.0Adapter contributes at "natural" magnitude
$\alpha = 2r$ (our winner)22.0Adapter contributes 2× stronger
$\alpha < r$< 1< 1.0Damped, conservative updates
Our Phase 2 config: rank = 4, $\alpha$ = 8 (ratio 2). The adapter's output is multiplied by $8/4 = 2.0$ before adding to the frozen model's output. This means the adapter learns aggressively — each update has twice the "natural" impact.

In practice, $\alpha$ acts like a learning rate multiplier specific to the adapter. A high ratio pushes the model further from pretrained behavior per step; a low ratio keeps it closer. The key insight: $\alpha$ and LR are partially redundant — doubling $\alpha$ has a similar effect to doubling LR for the adapter parameters. The convention of expressing $\alpha$ as a ratio of rank (rather than an absolute value) ensures this knob stays meaningful when you change rank.

The flaw in $1/r$: The normalization assumes $\|BA\|$ scales linearly with $r$. But from random matrix theory, the expected norm actually scales with $\sqrt{r}$. This means $1/r$ over-corrects — it makes high-rank adapters weaker than intended. This is exactly what RsLoRA fixes.

RsLoRA (Rank-Stabilized LoRA)

RsLoRA fixes the over-correction in standard LoRA's scaling by replacing $\alpha/r$ with $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$:

$$h = Wx + \frac{\alpha}{\sqrt{r}} \cdot BAx$$

Since $\|BA\|$ scales with $\sqrt{r}$ (not $r$), dividing by $\sqrt{r}$ is the correct normalization to keep the adapter's contribution constant across ranks.

Reading this chart — "Scaling Factor": This is the multiplier ($\alpha/r$ or $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$) applied after the raw adapter output. It answers: "how much does the normalization dampen the adapter at each rank?" The effective adapter contribution is raw output magnitude × scaling factor — ideally this product is constant across ranks (so rank controls capacity, not step size). Notice how standard scaling (burgundy) collapses toward zero at high ranks, while RsLoRA (blue) stays much flatter — that's the correct behavior.

Practical Impact

The gradient collapse problem: With standard scaling, a rank-32 adapter receives 8× smaller effective gradients than rank-4. This means higher-rank experiments need proportionally higher learning rates to compensate — making rank and LR deeply entangled in HP sweeps.

RsLoRA decouples rank from effective learning rate, giving two benefits:

BenefitExplanation
Fairer rank comparisonSweeps over rank become apples-to-apples — you no longer need to co-tune LR for each rank value
Better high-rank trainingRank-16 and rank-32 adapters can actually leverage their capacity instead of being gradient-starved

In PEFT, it's a one-flag change: set use_rslora=True in LoraConfig. No other code changes required.

Pros and Cons of RsLoRA

Advantages
Decouples rank from LRGradient magnitude stays stable across ranks, so a single learning rate works for rank-4 through rank-64. Eliminates the need for a 2D grid sweep (rank × LR).
Unlocks high-rank adaptersRank-16 and rank-32 can actually leverage their capacity instead of being gradient-starved. Standard scaling suppresses rank-32 gradients by $5.66\times$ relative to RsLoRA.
Fairer HP comparisonsWhen the sweep converges on rank-4, you can trust it's because rank-4 has the right capacity — not because higher ranks were artificially crippled by scaling.
Zero-cost adoptionOne boolean flag in PEFT. No architectural changes, no additional compute, no new hyperparameters.
Theoretically groundedDerived from random matrix theory ($\|BA\| \propto \sqrt{r}$), not an empirical heuristic.
Risks and Limitations
LR re-tuning neededExisting LR values were tuned under standard $\alpha/r$ scaling. Switching to $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$ effectively multiplies the adapter's contribution by $\sqrt{r}$, so the old LR may be too aggressive. Our Phase 2 winner (LR $\approx 10^{-3}$, rank-4) would see a $2\times$ stronger adapter under RsLoRA — may need to halve LR or reduce $\alpha$.
Not always beneficial at low rankAt rank-4, the difference between $\alpha/r$ and $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$ is only $2\times$. The benefit grows with rank. If your sweep converges on low rank anyway, RsLoRA adds little.
Breaks comparability with prior runsResults under RsLoRA are not directly comparable to results under standard scaling at the same $\alpha$ value, because the effective scaling differs. Requires re-running baselines or carefully adjusting $\alpha$.
Assumption: random initializationThe $\sqrt{r}$ norm argument assumes $B$ and $A$ have random independent entries. If adapters are initialized from a pretrained checkpoint or structured init, the actual norm growth may differ from $\sqrt{r}$.
Less community validationStandard $\alpha/r$ has been the default since the original LoRA paper (2021). RsLoRA is newer and less battle-tested across diverse tasks and architectures.
