Overview

As pretrained large language models replace task-specific decoders in speech recognition, a critical question arises: do their text-derived priors make recognition fairer or more biased across demographic groups?

We evaluated nine ASR models spanning three architectural generations on ~43,000 utterances across five demographic axes (ethnicity, accent, gender, age, first language) using Common Voice 24 and Meta's Fair-Speech. We then stress-tested all models under 12 acoustic degradation conditions across both datasets, totaling 216 inference runs. The midterm paper, "Do LLM Decoders Listen Fairly?", is under review at COLM 2026.

Why Fair-Speech matters

Because all speakers read the same prompted sentences, any WER differences are attributable to acoustic or model factors, not vocabulary confounds. This makes Fair-Speech the primary dataset for isolating demographic bias. To our knowledge, we report the first comprehensive multi-model benchmark on Fair-Speech.

Processed manifests

Raw datasets are normalized into standardized CSV manifests for the inference pipeline. Demographic labels are shortened (e.g., "Black or African American" → "Black/AA", "United States English" → "us"), gender is standardized, and age brackets are cleaned. Each manifest row contains an utterance ID, absolute audio path, normalized reference text, and all available demographic columns for that dataset.

Perturbations

Real-world speech is rarely clean. Degraded signals force the decoder to compensate from its language prior, potentially revealing demographic biases invisible on clean benchmarks. We tested all models under 4 perturbation types at 3 severity levels:

PerturbationLevelsSource
Additive noiseSNR 20 / 10 / 0 dBMUSAN noise corpus
ReverberationRT60 0.3 / 0.6 / 1.0 sOpenSLR RIRs
Silence injection25 / 50 / 75% of durationSynthetic (random position)
Chunk masking10 / 20 / 30% zeroedSynthetic (multiple chunks)

Noise and reverb degrade signal quality while preserving temporal structure. Silence and masking remove acoustic information entirely, maximally forcing decoder reliance on its language prior. Total: 9 models × 12 conditions × 2 datasets = 216 inference runs.

Metrics

Primary accuracy metric: Word Error Rate (WER). Primary fairness metric: max/min ratio (MMR):

$$\text{MMR} = \frac{\max_{g \in G}\, \text{WER}_g}{\min_{g \in G}\, \text{WER}_g}$$

where $G$ is the set of demographic groups with $n \geq 50$. $\text{MMR} = 1.0$ is perfect parity; preferred over absolute gap because it is scale-invariant. All group-level WERs include 95% bootstrap CIs (200 resamples).

Error decomposition following Morris et al. (2004): substitutions, insertions, and deletions — distinguishing acoustic confusion from decoder-driven hallucinations. Insertions further classified into repetition (autoregressive loops), syntactic (function words), and content (semantic fabrications).

Perturbation fairness measured by amplification ratio:

$$\alpha = \frac{\text{MMR}_\text{pert}}{\text{MMR}_\text{clean}}$$

where $\alpha > 1$ means degradation widens demographic gaps and $\alpha < 1$ means compression.

Key Findings

Ethnicity fairness

  1. Black/AA speakers have the highest WER across all 9 models, regardless of generation or architecture. Statistically significant (non-overlapping 95% CIs) for 8 of 9 models.
  2. LLM decoders do not amplify racial bias. Granite-8B (Gen 3, MMR = 2.28) achieves the best ethnicity fairness among competitive models, outperforming all three Whisper models (MMR 3.13–4.04).
  3. Measurement paradox: Qwen3-1.7B achieves the lowest absolute Black/AA WER (8.45%) yet the highest relative gap (+203%) because its White WER is very low (2.79%). Practitioners should report both absolute WER per group and relative disparity.

Accent fairness and hallucination

  1. Audio compression predicts accent fairness more than LLM scale. Low-compression Qwen3-1.7B (accent MMR = 1.82) outperforms high-compression Granite-2B (MMR = 3.30) at comparable parameter count.
  2. Whisper hallucinates on Indian-accented speech with a non-monotonic insertion rate: small 3.22% → medium 1.53% → large-v3 9.62% (50.7% of all errors). All Gen 3 models stay below 3.1% on the same utterances.
  3. Hallucination breakdown at the 9.62% rate (495 insertions on 511 utterances): 43% repetition loops, 48% syntactic completions, 9.3% content hallucinations (e.g., "grow sea monkeys" → "go and see what happens").

Robustness under degradation

  1. Severe degradation paradoxically compresses fairness gaps. Under 30% masking, all models' ethnicity MMR compresses ($\alpha = 0.36\text{-}0.79$) — "low-accuracy parity" where all groups converge to high WER.
  2. Exception: silence amplifies Whisper's accent bias up to $4.64\times$ ($\text{MMR}: 3.23 \to 15.0$ at 25% silence for Whisper-small), driven by demographic-selective hallucination.
  3. Hallucination type is architecture-dependent. Under 30% masking, Whisper-small produces 51,797 insertions (86% repetition loops). Qwen3-1.7B produces 1,353 ($38\times$ fewer, 3% repetition). High-compression Granite re-introduces Whisper-like repetition (49–57%) even with an explicit LLM decoder.
Bottom line: Qwen3-1.7B dominates on accuracy, accent fairness, and robustness. Granite-8B is preferable for ethnicity fairness. Audio encoder design, not LLM scaling, is the primary lever for equitable and robust speech recognition.

Next Steps

The midterm findings motivate a fairness-aware fine-tuning intervention for Qwen3-ASR-1.7B using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with a composite accuracy-fairness reward:

$$R = (1 - \lambda)\,(1 - \text{WER}) \;+\; \lambda\bigl(-\lvert \text{WER}_g - \overline{\text{WER}} \rvert\bigr)$$

We have prepared speaker-level stratified train/eval splits for Fair-Speech (80/20, ~21K/5.3K utterances, stratified by ethnicity with disjoint speakers) and a demographic-aware data loading pipeline. The remaining work: LoRA adapter integration, GRPO training loop, three fairness baselines (FairLoRA, Group-DRO, ICASSP 2026 fairness-prompted), and a $\lambda$ sweep to trace the accuracy–fairness Pareto frontier.
