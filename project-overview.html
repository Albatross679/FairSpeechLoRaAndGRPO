1. Overview

As pretrained large language models replace task-specific decoders in speech recognition, a critical question arises: do their text-derived priors make recognition fairer or more biased across demographic groups?

We evaluated nine ASR models spanning three architectural generations on ~43,000 utterances across five demographic axes (ethnicity, accent, gender, age, first language) using Common Voice 25 and Meta's Fair-Speech. We then stress-tested all models under 12 acoustic degradation conditions across both datasets, totaling 216 inference runs. The midterm paper, "Do LLM Decoders Listen Fairly?", is under review at COLM 2026.

Why Fair-Speech matters

All speakers read from a shared pool of prompted sentences (23,045 unique sentences across 26,471 utterances), so vocabulary is largely controlled and WER differences are primarily attributable to acoustic or model factors, not lexical confounds. This makes Fair-Speech the primary dataset for isolating demographic bias. To our knowledge, we report the first comprehensive multi-model benchmark on Fair-Speech.

Processed manifests

Raw datasets are normalized into standardized CSV manifests for the inference pipeline: cv_train.csv (Common Voice), ls_test_clean.csv (LibriSpeech), and Fair-Speech manifests. Demographic labels are shortened (e.g., "Black or African American" → "Black/AA", "United States English" → "us"), gender is standardized, and age brackets are cleaned. Each manifest row contains an utterance ID, speaker_id (where available), absolute audio path, normalized reference text, and all available demographic columns for that dataset.

3. Midterm Findings (COLM 2026)

The midterm paper, "Do LLM Decoders Listen Fairly?" (Ginjala & Wen, under review at COLM 2026), benchmarked nine ASR models across three architectural generations on ~43,000 utterances under clean and degraded conditions. The methodology and key results are summarized below.

Perturbations

Real-world speech is rarely clean. Degraded signals force the decoder to compensate from its language prior, potentially revealing demographic biases invisible on clean benchmarks. We tested all models under 4 perturbation types at 3 severity levels:

PerturbationLevelsSource
Additive noiseSNR 20 / 10 / 0 dBMUSAN noise corpus
ReverberationRT60 0.3 / 0.6 / 1.0 sOpenSLR RIRs
Silence injection25 / 50 / 75% of durationSynthetic (random position)
Chunk masking10 / 20 / 30% zeroedSynthetic (multiple chunks)

Noise and reverb degrade signal quality while preserving temporal structure. Silence and masking remove acoustic information entirely, maximally forcing decoder reliance on its language prior. Total: 9 models × 12 conditions × 2 datasets = 216 inference runs.

Metrics

Primary accuracy metric: Word Error Rate (WER). Primary fairness metric: max/min ratio (MMR):

$$\text{MMR} = \frac{\max_{g \in G}\, \text{WER}_g}{\min_{g \in G}\, \text{WER}_g}$$
  • $G$ = demographic groups with $n \geq 50$
  • $\text{MMR} = 1.0$ is perfect parity; preferred over absolute gap because it is scale-invariant
  • All group-level WERs include 95% bootstrap CIs (200 resamples)
Error decomposition (Morris et al., 2004)
  • Substitutions — acoustic confusion
  • Deletions — missed words
  • Insertions — decoder-driven hallucinations, further classified:
    • Repetition — autoregressive loops
    • Syntactic — function words
    • Content — semantic fabrications
Perturbation fairness — is the model's fairness real or fragile?

Clean audio is easy — the model barely needs its language prior. Degraded audio forces that reliance, surfacing demographic biases hidden under clean conditions.

$$\alpha = \frac{\text{MMR}_\text{pert}}{\text{MMR}_\text{clean}}$$
  • $\alpha = 1$ — no fairness change
  • $\alpha > 1$ — degradation widens demographic gaps
  • $\alpha < 1$ — degradation compresses gaps (often "low-accuracy parity")

Key findings

Ethnicity fairness
  1. Black/AA speakers have the highest WER across all 9 models, regardless of generation or architecture. Statistically significant (non-overlapping 95% CIs) for 8 of 9 models.
  2. LLM decoders do not amplify racial bias. Granite-8B (Gen 3, MMR = 2.28) achieves the best ethnicity fairness among competitive models, outperforming all three Whisper models (MMR 3.13–4.04).
  3. Measurement paradox: Qwen3-1.7B achieves the lowest absolute Black/AA WER (8.45%) yet the highest relative gap (+203%) because its White WER is very low (2.79%). Practitioners should report both absolute WER per group and relative disparity.
Accent fairness and hallucination
  1. Audio compression predicts accent fairness more than LLM scale. Low-compression Qwen3-1.7B (accent MMR = 1.82) outperforms high-compression Granite-2B (MMR = 3.30) at comparable parameter count.
  2. Whisper hallucinates on Indian-accented speech with a non-monotonic insertion rate: small 3.22% → medium 1.53% → large-v3 9.62% (50.7% of all errors). All Gen 3 models stay below 3.1% on the same utterances.
  3. Hallucination breakdown at the 9.62% rate (495 insertions on 511 utterances): 43% repetition loops, 48% syntactic completions, 9.3% content hallucinations (e.g., "grow sea monkeys" → "go and see what happens").
Robustness under degradation
  1. Severe degradation paradoxically compresses fairness gaps. Under 30% masking, all models' ethnicity MMR compresses ($\alpha = 0.36\text{-}0.79$) — "low-accuracy parity" where all groups converge to high WER.
  2. Exception: silence amplifies Whisper's accent bias up to $4.64\times$ ($\text{MMR}: 3.23 \to 15.0$ at 25% silence for Whisper-small), driven by demographic-selective hallucination.
  3. Hallucination type is architecture-dependent. Under 30% masking, Whisper-small produces 51,797 insertions (86% repetition loops). Qwen3-1.7B produces 1,353 ($38\times$ fewer, 3% repetition). High-compression Granite re-introduces Whisper-like repetition (49–57%) even with an explicit LLM decoder.
Bottom line: Qwen3-1.7B dominates on accuracy, accent fairness, and robustness. Granite-8B is preferable for ethnicity fairness. Audio encoder design, not LLM scaling, is the primary lever for equitable and robust speech recognition.

4. Standard LoRA Baseline

Target model: Qwen3-ASR-1.7B

We fine-tune Qwen3-ASR-1.7B (Qwen, 2025), a Generation 3 speech model that routes audio embeddings directly into a pretrained LLM decoder. It achieved the best accuracy in our midterm evaluation (4.73% WER on Fair-Speech, 7.76% on Common Voice) while maintaining competitive fairness, making it the natural target for fairness-aware fine-tuning.

HP sweep: PLoRA + ASHA + RsLoRA

We used three techniques to run a large-scale HP sweep on a single T4:

  • PLoRA (Packed LoRA) — attach N parallel LoRA adapters to a single frozen base model and train them simultaneously via round-robin gradient steps, eliminating redundant model loading
  • ASHA (Asynchronous Successive Halving) — early-stop unpromising trials at rung checkpoints {25, 50, 75} steps, keeping only the top 1/3 per rung
  • RsLoRA (rank-stable scaling) — replace the standard LoRA scaling $\alpha/r$ with $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$, decoupling rank from effective learning rate

220 Optuna trials total (TPE sampler, ask/tell API) across 9 HP dimensions, each training for 100 steps on a 500-sample stratified subset (Fair-Speech + Common Voice). Gradient isolation was verified before sweeping (max parameter diff = 0.0 across 224 params). Total wall-clock: ~13 hours, peak VRAM 9.7 GB.

RsLoRA effect on rank preference

Standard LoRA scales the adapter update by $\alpha/r$, so doubling rank halves the effective update magnitude. RsLoRA uses $\alpha/\sqrt{r}$ instead, softening this penalty. The effect on the sweep was clear:

Top-10 rank distributionrank 4rank 8rank 32
Without RsLoRA820
With RsLoRA271

RsLoRA shifted the optimal rank from 4 to 8 by letting higher-rank adapters retain more gradient signal. However, the best absolute loss was still achieved without RsLoRA (0.142 vs 0.145). Since we already swept alpha ratio alongside rank, the sweep could find the right scaling either way—RsLoRA is a convenience for rank-agnostic tuning, not a strict improvement when alpha is co-optimized.

Baseline summary: The sweep-optimized LoRA adapter achieves strong accuracy (1.85–3.15% WER) with improved fairness over the initial config — the ethnicity max-min ratio dropped from 3.43 to 1.94. 5. Next Steps

The midterm findings motivate a fairness-aware fine-tuning intervention for Qwen3-ASR-1.7B using Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) with a composite accuracy-fairness reward:

$$R = (1 - \lambda)\,(1 - \text{WER}) \;+\; \lambda\bigl(-\lvert \text{WER}_g - \overline{\text{WER}} \rvert\bigr)$$

We train on both Fair-Speech and Common Voice combined (equal split), with speaker-disjoint train/eval splits where speaker IDs are available (Common Voice client_id; Fair-Speech lacks speaker grouping). A standard LoRA baseline (lambda=0) has been trained and evaluated, establishing per-group WER baselines across ethnicity and accent axes. The remaining work: GRPO training loop, three fairness baselines (FairLoRA, Group-DRO, ICASSP 2026 fairness-prompted), and a $\lambda$ sweep to trace the accuracy–fairness Pareto frontier.
