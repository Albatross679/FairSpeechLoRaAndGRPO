Attention Head Surgery — v2.0 Workflow Explainer

A plain-language guide before you make scoping decisions

Why this document exists. Milestone v2.0 pivots from GRPO (v1.0, paused) to attention head surgery on Whisper-large-v3. Before I ask you to decide on MVP scope, regression guards, sweep scope, and driving-ness thresholds, this page explains what those terms mean and why each choice matters. Read sections 1–4, then the decision cards in section 5.

1. The Problem

Your midterm found a specific failure mode of Whisper-large-v3. It's the most accurate ASR model tested — but on Indian-accented English speech, it hallucinates words that weren't spoken.

Whisper-large-v3 on Indian-accent Common Voice (n=511, from midterm §3.2)

The reference methodology: Calm-Whisper

A paper from Interspeech 2025 (Wang et al., "Calm-Whisper") showed that on non-speech audio (where Whisper hallucinates text out of silence), just 3 of 20 decoder self-attention heads drive 75% of the hallucinations. Fine-tuning just those 3 heads reduced hallucination by 80% with almost no degradation on LibriSpeech.

v2.0 adapts that methodology from non-speech hallucination to accent hallucination. Same model (Whisper-large-v3), same mechanism (per-head analysis), different failure mode.

Caveat. Calm-Whisper's "3 of 20 heads" finding was on a different dataset and a different type of hallucination. The same heads may or may not be responsible for accent hallucination. The v2.0 contribution is finding out, not assuming.

2. What's an Attention Head?

Think of Whisper's decoder as a factory with 32 floors. Each floor has 20 specialist workers (attention heads). Each worker looks at a different slice of the previous words + audio and decides what to focus on. Their outputs are averaged and passed up to the next floor.

DimensionCountIn plain language
Decoder layers3232 floors in the factory
Self-attention heads per layer2020 specialists per floor
Total positions640640 workers total (32 × 20)

What "masking a head" means

To test if a head is hallucination-driving, we set its contribution to zero — the worker is still at their desk but their output is discarded. Then we re-run inference and measure: does the hallucination rate drop?

If masking head h ...Then h is ...
Drops insertion rate (9.62% → 4%) and doesn't hurt other accentshallucination-driving — a candidate for surgery
Changes nothingneutral — ignore it
Drops insertion rate BUT hurts WER on other accentsdoing useful work elsewhere — don't touch it
Raises insertion rateactually preventing hallucination — definitely don't touch it

3. The 4-Arm Workflow

v2.0 has four possible interventions. They're independent — you can do any subset. The primary contribution is Arm 1 (Diagnosis); the others are side-quests or stretch.

Arm 1 — Primary

Diagnosis Sweep

What: Mask each of the 640 heads one at a time, re-run inference on 511 Indian-accent utterances, measure which heads drop insertion rate.

Output: A ranked list of hallucination-driving heads (a.k.a. top-k heads) + a 32×20 heatmap for the writeup.

Why primary: This alone is a complete, citable contribution — independent replication of Calm-Whisper on a new failure mode.

Arm 2 — Side-quest

Decoding Ablation

What: Try cheap decoder tweaks — beam size, repetition_penalty, no_repeat_ngram_size. No training, just re-run inference with different decoding settings.

Output: A grid of "what if we just turned this knob?" numbers.

Purpose: Answers the unavoidable reviewer question "couldn't you just set repetition_penalty=1.3 and call it a day?" with real numbers.

Arm 3 — Side-quest

VAD Preprocessing

What: Run a voice-activity detector (silero-vad) over the audio FIRST, strip out silence, THEN feed the trimmed audio to Whisper.

Output: Insertion rate on the same 511 utterances with/without VAD, plus interaction with the existing silence-injection perturbations (25/50/75%).

Purpose: Tests whether silence is the trigger. Requested by midterm §4.2.

Arm 4 — Stretch

Selective Fine-Tuning

What: Take the top-k heads identified in Arm 1 and fine-tune ONLY those heads on clean accent-diverse audio. Everything else in the model stays frozen.

Output: A fine-tuned checkpoint that reduces Indian-accent insertions without hurting other accents.

Why stretch: Can't start until Arm 1 is done, takes the most time, and carries the most risk (head collapse, catastrophic forgetting, overfit at n=306 training samples).

4. The "Ship Diagnosis First" Principle

Bottom line. Arm 1 (Diagnosis) is a complete contribution on its own. If only one thing ships, it must be Arm 1. If Arm 1 finishes early, add Arms 2 / 3 / 4 in that priority order. If Arm 1 is late, cut everything else.

5. Decisions You Need to Make

Four scoping decisions drive the REQUIREMENTS document. Each card explains the plain-language question, the options, and why one option is recommended.

Decision 1 — Is fine-tuning (Arm 4) required or stretch?

Do we commit to delivering a fine-tuned model, or do we try to deliver one if Arm 1 finishes early?

StretchREC
Arm 4 is optional. MVP = Arms 1 + 2 + 3. If Arm 1 finishes early, add Arm 4. If Arm 1 is late, cut Arm 4 entirely. Why: Arm 4 can't start until Arm 1 publishes the head ranking — it's always on the critical path. Committing to it forces Arm 1 to ship fast, which undermines rigor.
Required (MVP)
Arm 4 must ship. MVP = Arms 1 + 4. Trade-off: If Arm 1 slips by a week, Arm 4 has no time to train/evaluate and you deliver a broken result. Pick this only if you're confident Arm 1 will finish with runway to spare.

Decision 2 — Which non-Indian groups are the regression guard?

When you mask a head or fine-tune a model, you need to check that you didn't just trade Indian-accent improvement for regression on other accents. The "regression guard" is the accent(s) / dataset(s) you watch. If WER on the guard degrades by more than a fixed budget (e.g. 0.5 absolute points), the intervention fails.

LibriSpeech test-cleanREC
2,620 utterances, no demographic labels. Standard ASR reference; what Calm-Whisper used. Makes your result comparable to published work. Why: Every Whisper paper reports LibriSpeech — it's the lingua franca of ASR. Safest baseline guard.
CV24 American accent
Largest non-Indian CV group (~600 utterances). Stable across perturbations in the midterm. Natural complement to LibriSpeech for CV-specific regression.
CV24 Canadian accent
Stable in midterm; smaller n but clean baseline. Good secondary guard.
Fair-Speech "White" ethnicity
Best Whisper performance in midterm — acts as a ceiling check. If this degrades, something is badly broken.

This question is multi-select. Recommended combination: LibriSpeech + American + Canadian as three guards with 0.5 pt WER budget each.

Decision 3 — How many positions do we sweep in Arm 1?

The diagnosis sweep runs inference once per masked position. 640 positions means 640 inference runs on 511 utterances. Smaller sweep = faster but less informative.

Full 640 (all 32 layers × 20 heads)REC
Mask every possible (layer, head) position. Produces the 32×20 heatmap for the writeup. Roughly a few hours on a 49 GB GPU with encoder output caching. Why: Gives the most complete picture and cleanly tests whether Calm-Whisper's "heads 1, 6, 11" finding transfers to accent hallucination — which is the whole point.
Calm-Whisper heads only (3 × 32 = 96 positions)
Only mask heads 1, 6, 11 across all 32 layers. 6.6× faster. Trade-off: Assumes Calm-Whisper's finding transfers. If it doesn't, you won't know which heads ARE driving the accent hallucination — you can't independently rediscover them.
One representative layer (20 positions)
Pick one layer, sweep all 20 heads. 32× faster. Trade-off: Weak contribution — no cross-layer picture, no heatmap, not citable as a replication study.

Decision 4 — How do we decide a head is "hallucination-driving"?

After masking each head and measuring the change in insertion rate, you need a rule to classify heads as "driving" vs "not driving." This is the "driving-ness metric." Three styles:

Bootstrap 95% CI < 0REC
Run 1,000 bootstrap resamples of the insertion-rate delta. A head is "driving" only if the entire 95% confidence interval is below zero. Why: Statistically principled. Handles small-sample noise honestly. You can publish the CI alongside the ranking and defend the claim to reviewers. Key term: bootstrap CI — see glossary.
Absolute delta ≥ 1 percentage point
A head is "driving" if masking it drops insertion rate by ≥1 pp on the diagnose split. Trade-off: Easier to explain but reports effects that may just be noise at n=306. Could produce false positives.
Top-k ranking (k=3 or k=5)
Rank all 640 heads by insertion-rate delta, take the top k. Matches Calm-Whisper's style exactly ("3 of 20 heads"). Trade-off: Ignores statistical significance. The 4th-ranked head might be almost identical to the 3rd, or it might be pure noise — you can't tell from the ranking alone.

Hybrid option: use Bootstrap CI as the primary classifier AND report top-k as a secondary table for comparison with Calm-Whisper. This is the approach most papers take. Tell me if you want the hybrid and I'll write the requirement accordingly.

6. Watch Out For

Four traps are specific to this workflow. The first three are silent-failure landmines — the code looks like it worked, but the numbers are fake. The fourth is a class-project scope risk.

Pitfall 1 — SDPA silently ignores head_mask. HuggingFace defaults to a fast attention kernel called SDPA. SDPA does NOT support masking individual heads — it silently drops any mask you pass. The sweep runs, produces numbers, looks fine, but every masked run is identical to the baseline. The fix: explicitly set attn_implementation="eager" when loading Whisper, and assert it after load. Must be a hard gate in Phase A.
Pitfall 2 — KV cache may drop head_mask after token 1. Whisper generates tokens one at a time using a cache of previous attention. Passing decoder_head_mask as a .generate() kwarg may only apply the mask to the FIRST token; tokens 2..T come from the unmasked model. The fix: use PyTorch forward hooks that zero the head at EVERY forward call, and verify with output_attentions=True that the head is zero at decoded position > 1.
Pitfall 3 — Whisper's temperature fallback confounds decoding ablation. Whisper has a built-in safety net: if a transcription looks repetitive, it automatically re-runs the segment at a higher temperature. This means your "beam=5, rep_penalty=1.0" run may actually be executing at temperature 0.8 on the hardest utterances — exactly the ones you care about. The fix: explicitly set temperature=0.0, compression_ratio_threshold=None, logprob_threshold=None in Phase C.
Pitfall 4 — Scope creep. Four arms is too many for a class project. The plan lists 4 interventions. In a time-limited class project, four shallow arms is worse than one rigorous arm. Commit to Arm 1 as the required deliverable and declare 2, 3, 4 as stretch in PROJECT.md and REQUIREMENTS.md explicitly. The cut order under time pressure is Arm 2 → Arm 3 → Arm 4. Arm 1 is never cut.

7. Glossary

Attention head
A specialist sub-module of a transformer layer that attends to a slice of the context. Whisper-large-v3's decoder has 20 per layer × 32 layers = 640 total.
Bootstrap CI (confidence interval)
A statistical technique. Resample your data with replacement 1,000 times, compute the metric each time, take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles as the 95% confidence interval. If "0" is outside the interval, the effect is statistically significant.
Diagnose / rank / test split
Dividing the 511 Indian-accent utterances into three non-overlapping groups. "Diagnose" for the big 640-cell sweep, "rank" for tie-breaking the top-k heads, "test" touched exactly once at the end to report the final number. Prevents cherry-picking.
Driving-ness metric
The rule you use to decide "this head drives hallucination" vs "this head doesn't matter." See Decision 4 in section 5.
Eager attention
The oldest, slowest, most-flexible PyTorch attention implementation. The ONLY one that supports masking individual heads. Alternatives: SDPA (fast, no head mask), FlashAttention-2 (fastest, no head mask).
Hallucination (in ASR)
The model transcribes words that weren't spoken. Three subtypes: repetition ("the the the"), syntactic (filler function words), content (invented nouns/verbs). Not the same as substitution or deletion errors.
Insertion rate
Inserted words ÷ total reference words. Whisper-large-v3's insertion rate on Indian-accent CV is 9.62%. Lower is better.
KV cache
During autoregressive generation, the model caches its previous attention computations (key-value pairs) to avoid recomputing them for every new token. This cache is the source of Pitfall 2 — a head mask may be applied when the cache is built but not when it's read.
MMR (Max/Min Ratio)
Fairness metric: (highest WER across demographic groups) ÷ (lowest WER across groups). MMR = 1.0 means perfect parity. Whisper-large-v3's ethnicity MMR was 2.28 in the midterm.
Per-head masking
Setting a specific attention head's output to zero during inference to see if the model still works correctly without it. The core primitive of Arm 1.
Regression guard
An accent/dataset you watch to ensure the intervention doesn't break general performance. If the guard's WER regresses by more than a fixed budget (e.g. 0.5 absolute points), the intervention is rejected even if it helped the Indian-accent target.
SDPA (Scaled Dot-Product Attention)
PyTorch's default fast attention kernel. Does NOT support per-head masking — this is Pitfall 1. For this milestone you MUST override to eager attention.
Top-k ranking
A way to classify heads: rank all 640 by some score (e.g., insertion-rate delta) and take the top k (3 or 5). Matches Calm-Whisper's "3 of 20 heads" framing but ignores significance.
VAD (Voice Activity Detection)
A small model that classifies audio frames as speech vs silence/noise. In Arm 3, run VAD first to trim silence, then feed the trimmed audio to Whisper. Library recommended: silero-vad (not WebRTC — WebRTC deletes half the soft-spoken speech).
WER (Word Error Rate)
The primary ASR accuracy metric: (substitutions + deletions + insertions) ÷ reference word count. Lower is better. Whisper-large-v3 is the best on LibriSpeech but has the worst Indian-accent insertion behavior.
